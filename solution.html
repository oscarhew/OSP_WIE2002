Solutions

While poverty can manifest itself in different ways and have a range of causes, there are various strategies that can be implemented to alleviate and ultimately eradicate it. These solutions are often multi-faceted and may require coordinated efforts from different sectors and stakeholders, including governments, international organizations, non-governmental organizations, the private sector, and individuals.

Eliminating Poverty Through Equity

Policies that promote equity can help, directly and indirectly, to reduce poverty. When incomes are more evenly distributed, fewer individuals fall below the poverty line. Equity-enhancing policies, particularly such investment in human capital as education, can, in the long run, boost economic growth, which, in turn, has been shown to alleviate poverty.

Invest in affordable, accessible housing

The housing situation in many countries is characterized by significant disparities, with a large percentage of renters experiencing housing insecurity and cost-burdened. People of color and those with disabilities are particularly vulnerable to housing insecurity and discrimination in obtaining and maintaining housing. Homelessness is a significant issue, with chronic homelessness on the rise. Investments in permanent housing programs and renter protections, such as a right to counsel and regulating the use of background checks, can help address these issues and promote more inclusive communities.

Raise the minimum wage to ensure economic stability for all

Congress must raise the federal minimum wage, which has not been increased in more than a decade and is currently just $7.25 per hour. This is not enough to keep workers out of poverty, and the subminimum wage for tipped and disabled workers only exacerbates the problem. The Raise the Wage Act proposes a gradual increase to $15 per hour by 2025 and indexing it to median wage growth thereafter. It also phases out subminimum wages for tipped, teenage, and disabled workers. These changes would lift millions out of poverty and disproportionately benefit marginalized communities.



Create good-paying jobs that meet family needs

To build a more equitable economy, federal investment is needed to create new jobs through initiatives such as the American Jobs Plan, which proposes investing $2.3 trillion in areas such as green energy, caregiving, domestic manufacturing, and infrastructure. The plan aims to provide decent wages and benefits and make these jobs accessible to all Americans. If passed, it could help rebuild the economy and address the gap of 7.6 million jobs lost since February 2020, promoting financial security and savings for the future.



Expand safety net programs to benefit all in need

The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the inadequacies of America's safety net programs. Unemployment insurance (UI) and Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) have not been able to provide sufficient support to those in need. While temporary federal expansions have provided relief, many governors are cutting benefits, leaving millions in dire straits. The American Rescue Plan has made progress in expanding food assistance programs but eligibility for SNAP must be further expanded to include excluded groups. Lawmakers must invest in and modernize safety net programs and implement automatic triggers to expand benefits during future economic shocks. This would not only prevent people from falling into poverty but also stimulate the economy by giving timely relief to those in need
